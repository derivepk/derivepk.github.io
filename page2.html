Naran

At 7,888 feet above sea level, Naran is the main center of tourism in the Kaghan Valley. Around 85 kilometers from Balakot, the town is situated on the bank of Kunharriver, a paradise for trout ﬁshing.
Naran becomes alive only in summer starting in late May, when the snow melts and the road to Naran become passable. In winter majority of the population descends to warmer climates of Balakot and Mansehra. Mystic Lake Saiful Muluk, Lalazar, Lake Lulusar and Babusar Pass are popular destinations in Naran.

Islamabad - Naran-Khunerab - China Border

Islamabad- Khunjearab road Trip 24-29 June What to expect
Get ready for a trip of lifetime. Buckle your seat belts for a roller coaster ride. In next six days you will travel on the eighth wonder of the world -the Karakorum Highway, which starts from Havelian and winds through beautiful spots including Abbottabad, Mansehra, Balakot, Naran, Chilas, Gilgit, Hunza, and Khunjerab National park.
KKH connects China's Xinjiang region with Pakistan's Gilgit–Baltistan region in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa at an altitude of 4,693 m/15,397 ft.
During your journey you will pass through most amazing landscapes, lakes, glaciers, the world’s three famous mountain ranges — the Himalayas, the Karakorams and the Hindukush and valleys with breathtaking views.
The British Backpacker Society identifies Pakistan as its top travel destination, calling it “one of the friendliest countries on Earth, with mountain scenery that is beyond anyone’s wildest imagination.”
During summer you will spend one night at Naran, the fairytale valley - a tourist hub -famous for its scenic views and natural beauty.
Lake Saif-ul-Malook
Just six miles from Naran, Lake Saif- ul-Malook is one of the highest lakes of Pakistan, 10,578 feet about sea level. The only way to reach the lake is either on a donkey/mule or a 4 x 4 jeep because it’s located on the top of a steep hill.
The history of this exquisite lake is much famous and attracts the tourists from all over the world. The story is about a prince, Saif-ul-Malook, who fell in love with a fairy princess, Badi-ul-Jamal.
Saif-ul-Malook, a prince of Egypt, inherited a treasure from his forefathers, inscribed with two seals; one bearing the image of Said and the other one being that of Badi-ul-Jamal.
When Saif saw the picture of the fairy he immediately fell in love with her. He left his home to search for her, in his long journey he finally met a saint who gave him a Sulemani cap, which could take the prince to his desired place.
The saint told the prince that he would find the fairy in a lake but he had to pass several daunting exams and also pray in order to achieve her, as she was a fairy queen and prince was human. A human eye can never see a demon or fairy as they are “fire borne”, he was told.
After passing several tests on the night of 14th he saw extremely beautiful the fairy queen with dark black hairs and radiant eyes coming along with her maids towards the lake for bath.
She told the prince that she was trapped in a castle at Koh Qaf by a white giant for the past 10 years.
After listening to the story of the fairy queen, Saif took her and tried to escape from this valley. When the white giant came to know of this he created turbulences out of anger in this lake, as a result of which flood came in Kaghan Valley, the both had to shelter in a cave near the lake.
Legend has it that the Ansoo Lake in Kaghan Valley, a few miles away from Saif-ul-Malook, was created out of the tears of the white giant when he found out that the fairy was gone.
The prince and the fairy queen still live, to this day, in that cave and dance above the water surface on the 14th night of every lunar month.
In winters and early summer, when Babusar top is closed due to snow, you have to spend night at Chilas, a transit town in the ancient trade and pilgrimage route. It has Ladakh in the East, Kashgar in the North, Balakot towards South and Chitral towards West.
Kunhar River
Kunhar, river flows through the entire Naran, Kaghan, alhad, Balakot, Garhi, Habibullah and Dalola valley and it merges with Jhelum River right outside Muzaffarabad. Kunhar River is famous for its hydro power generation potential.
Indus river
Indus is one of the longest rivers in Asia, which originates in the Tibetan Palteau, and run through ladakh, India and enters Pakistan in Gilgit Baltistan area. It is the national river of Pakistan. During your journey you will see many colours of mighty Indus.
River Indus has a total drainage area exceeding 1,165,000 km2 (450,000 sq mi), its annual flow of around 243 km3(58 cu mi), make it twice of the Nile river and three times of tigris and Euphrates river combined.
Hunza
After seeing the amazing landscape, rivers glaciers and snowy peaks, just after passing Gilgit, you will enter the northern territory of Gilgit-Baltistan.
Situated on the riverbank and surrounded by glaciers and gorges, the Hunza Valley, often referred to as heaven on earth, enveloped in the grand Himalayas and the Karakoram mountain ranges, is home to snow leopards, markhors, ibexes, and red-striped foxes.
Here you will see a whole new spectrum of colours and the landscape, freshness in air, and ambiance, which cannot be described in words.
The villagers are very friendly and eager to help. They will make you feel at home.
Despite severe shortage of plane land, Hunza people developed their agriculture system and have grown the best crop and yes, Hunza fruits are known as most delicious and unique. Watered with fresh water the organic apricots, cherry, nectarine, peach, apple, plum berry, grapes and pears have a unique taste, richness and nutritional value.
According to many western researchers the secret of longetivity of Hunza people is its fruits and fresh water.
Next to Hunza – amid rapidly changing landscape -blackish and forsaken mountains of Karakoram- there is this majestic lake, which came into being purely by chance.
Attabad Lake’s vibrant turquoise waters came into being when when a massive landslide at Attabad Village, blocked the flow of the Hunza River, in 2010.
From Hunza onwards the entire area is called Upper Hunza or Gojal. The Gojal Valley borders China and Afghanistan, with its border meeting the Chinese border at Khunjerab — 15,397 feet above sea level — and remains covered with snow all year long.
Before 1974, Gojal was part of the Hunza State, and was governed by the Mir of Hunza.
Gojal’s Gulmit Village was the summer capital of Hunza, where the Mir of Hunza used to hold his court.
Local music and dance programs were held here along with polo matches in spacious rectangular ground in front of the palace.
The Mir of Hunza announced an affiliation with Pakistan, but it enjoyed autonomy as a separate state till 1974.
In Z. A Bhutto’s era, Gojal became a Tehsil in the subdivision Hunza of Gilgit-Baltistan. Locally, it is also known as Upper Hunza, but officially it is Gojal.
In the north west of Gojal, is Chiporsun, which border the Wakhan region of Afghanistan.
After Wakhan, Tajikistan starts. It is a narrow strip of territory in Afghanistan that extends to China and separates Tajikistan from Afghanistan. Historically it acted as a buffer between the Russian empire and British empire (present Pakistan). Its eastern end bordered China's Xinjiang region, ruled by the Qing dynasty.
Dry port Sost or sust is the last village in Gojal on KKH, before Chinese border.
Before China border, is Pakistan's third largest national park,
Khunjerab National Park, established to protect Marco Polo shep and snow leopards.
The park was formally established in 1979 by the Prime Minister ZA Bhutto, who wanted to convert it into a world famous park.
The final destination of the journey is pak- China border at Khunjerab pass, or darra-e-khunjrab in urdu.
Its name is derived from two words of the local wakhi language. Khun' means Blood and 'Jerav' means a creek coming from spring water.
The Khunjerab Pass is the highest paved international border crossing in the world and the highest point on the Karakorum Highway or KKH. The road across the pass was completed in 1982. The Pakistani segment of the Karakoram Highway,starts from here.
The area's breathtaking raw beauty, rich heritage and its citizens' eagerness to keep their culture alive coupled with their kind hospitality destroy all the stereotypes Pakistan has set for itself.

Swat Valley

Swat- the Switzerland of Pakistan is famous for its scenic beauty. From the second century BC to the ninth century AD, Swat was cradle of Buddhism where at one time more than 1400 monasteries flourished
With its roaring rivers, waterfalls, meandering streams, glacier fed lakes, pine forests, alpine meadows, snow covered peaks of Mankial and Flaksair, fruit laden orchards, lush green fields, flower filled mountain slopes and its friendly people make Swat their one of the most beautiful tourist destination in the northern valleys.
Mankial, Mataltan Mashigram, Lake daral, Mahodand and Malam Jabba ski resort are some popular destinations in swat valley

Kashmir

A land of roaring rivers and majestic lakes, transparent streams and nullahs , snowy peaks and green mountains, forests and splendid valleys - Kashmir is a paradise on earth. The nature at its best lures the travelers from far and near to explore its beauty.  
Muzaffarabad, Toli Pir , Pir Chinsai, Neelum Valley, Sharda , Neelum Valley, Kel, Neelum Valley, Arangkel, Taobut, Leepa Valley, Chitta Katha Lake, Ratti Gali Lake, Shounter Lake, Gattian Lake, and Banjosa Lake are famous tourist destinations.

Bahawalpur

Bahawalpur, around 700 kilometers from Islamabad is situated in Punjab province. The princely state was founded by Nawab Muhammad Bahawal Khan-II in the early nineteenth century. In the United India, Bahawalpur was called the Rajputana State which is now Rajhistan in India.
 
The city is renowned for its many famous palaces which include Noor Mahal, Darbar Mahal Sadiq Ghar Palace.
The city’s location itself has historical significance as it is situated near the historical and ancient city of Uch as well as close to Harappa, both of which used to be a stronghold of the Delhi Sultanate and Indus Valley Civilization respectively. The Bahawalpur city also has a vast safari park, the Lal Suhanra National Park.
 
Derawar Fort

Around 100 kilometeers away from Bahawalpur is the ancient Derawar Fort. It was built in the 9th century under the kingship of Rai Jajja Bhati, a Hindu Rajput from Jaisalmir in India's Rajasthan state.
With 30-metre-high bastions surround the fort, along with its walls, that span over 1.5 kilometres, it is an engineering marvel.
In the harsh climate of the desert, the fort's red-bricked facade seems to glow with the heat and is visible from miles, changing colours as you approach.
The Derawar fort was also connected to other forts in Cholistan through a network of underground tunnels. On the ground floor, there were offices, a small prison, a water pond and residential quarters.
The fort attracts a large number of local, national and international tourists in the winters.
Since 2005, Cholistan car rally has evolved into a popular sport event.
The best time to visit Bahawalpur or Cholistan is in winters.
