Buddhist Sites 

Historians believe Buddhism came to Pakistan around 2,300 years ago, under King Ashoka. Budhists of the region gradually moved to Laddakh, and recently there were only 1884 registered Buddh voters in Pakistan as per National Database and Registration Authority’s 2017 record.
There is only one functional Buddhist temple in the Diplomatic Enclave, Islamabad, which is used by Buddhist diplomats.
However, the country has numerous Buddhist archaeological sites.
With its origin in 1000 B.C, Taxila is an important archaeological site of the ancient Indian subcontinent. There are over 50 archaeological sites scattered in a radius of 30 km around Taxila, including the Dharmarajika Stupa, Juulian, and Mohra Muradu Monastry. In 2006 Taxila was declared a Unesco world heritage site.
In Swat there were around 1400 monasteries in past. According to historians, It was the home of the famous Gandhara School of Sculpture, an expression of Graeco-Roman form in the local Buddhist tradition. Manthal Buddha Rocks Skardu, and Sacred rocks of Hunza, Shingardar Stupa near Bariko Mingora, and stupas in Amaan Kot and Jehan-a-Abad are considered great assets.
There are numerous other ancient archeological heritage sites in Pakistan, including Peshawar, Swat, Takht Bahi, southern Punjab, Sindh and Balouchistan.
